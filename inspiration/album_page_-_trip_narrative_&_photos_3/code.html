Kyoto in Autumn

A pilgrimage into silence, color, and the ancient capital's enduring spirit.

arrow_downward

The air was crisp, carrying the scent of cedar and impending rain. We arrived just as the leaves began their transformation, painting the ancient capital in deliberate hues of crimson and gold. This wasn't just a trip; it was a slow exhale after a year of holding our breath.

Kyoto doesn't reveal itself all at once. It asks for patience. It hides its best gardens behind unassuming walls and tucks its finest shrines at the end of long, winding paths. Over ten days, we walked the city's pulse, moving from the neon-soaked alleys of Pontocho to the silent bamboo groves of Arashiyama.

Part II

Into the Mountains

Leaving the city center behind, we took the train north. The landscape shifted rapidly from concrete density to sprawling green valleys. We spent three days hiking the trails around Kurama, where the roots of ancient cedars form natural staircases up the mountainside.

It rains differently here. It's not a downpour, but a mist that clings to everything, saturating the moss and turning the stone pathways slick and dark. We found refuge in a small tea house overlooking the river, warming our hands on ceramic cups of matcha.
